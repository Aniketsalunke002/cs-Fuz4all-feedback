# Fuzz4All Error-Guided Repair: Progress Summary
## 1. Initial Project Scope & Midterm
We extended the Fuzz4All (ICSE '24) paper by introducing an **error-guided repair stage**. The original fuzzer simply discarded C++ programs that failed to compile. Our extension takes the failed program, passes it alongside the compiler's `stderr` feedback to an LLM, and attempts to fix the syntax/type errors to increase the overall validity rate of the fuzzing corpus.
We also built:
- A structured evaluator script to score candidates.
- An AI-driven search harness (`run_search_round.py`) to systematically find the best repair prompt and configuration.
**Midterm Results:** Our initial headline result (StarCoderBase-7B, N=200) showed a significant improvement: the valid rate jumped from **60.5% (baseline)** to **73.5% (with repair)**.

## 2. Professor's Feedback on the Draft
Our professor reviewed the initial work and provided the following feedback for the final submission:
1. **Search Coverage:** Only 3 candidates were used in the initial search round. Expand to 5 or more.
2. **Instruction vs. Completion:** Add an "instruction-style" repair baseline to empirically prove that completion-style prompts work better for this model (good for ICSE/ISSTA publication).
3. **Verbose Logging:** Show exactly what the AI receives each round (the failed program, the compiler error, and the exact repair instruction).
4. **Compute Safety:** Add an explicit LLM call budget as a hard stop (not just limiting the number of rounds).
5. **Limitations:** Discuss "semantic correctness" as a limitation, acknowledging that `g++ -c` only checks if the program compiles, not if it retained its original logic.

## 3. What We Have Completed Today (The Full-Scale Run)
To address the **Search Coverage** and **Instruction Baseline** feedback, we executed a massive, full-scale search round on the HPC cluster.
**The Run Parameters:**
- **Model:** StarCoderBase-7B
- **Scale:** 10 different candidates 
- **Budget:** N=200 programs per candidate
- **Repeats:** 2 full runs per candidate to ensure statistical significance
**The Candidates Tested:**
We thoroughly explored the repair policy space, including:
- `r3_baseline_no_repair` (Control)
- `r3_I1_a2_instruction` (The requested instruction-style baseline)
- Temperature ablations (`T1` at 0.3, 0.7, 1.0)
- Max attempts ablations (1, 2, and 3 attempts)
- Alternative completion templates (`T2`, `T3`)
- Wide error gating (repairing all non-timeout errors instead of just compile errors)
**The Results:**
The experiment ran successfully overnight and yielded incredible data. 
1. **Repair Works:** The baseline valid rate was **62.25%**. The best repair policy achieved **91.00%**.
2. **Completion vs Instruction Proved:** The standard completion template (`T1`) achieved **87.5%**, completely outperforming the new instruction baseline (`I1`), which only got **74.5%**. This perfectly answers the professor's publication suggestion.
3. **New Best Policy Discovered:** We discovered that a "wide gate" (repairing all errors except timeouts) is the most effective policy, yielding the 91% validity rate.
